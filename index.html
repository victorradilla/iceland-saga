REYKJAVÍK 12 NIGHTS
PROLOGUE

A month the sagas remember.

Late May is when Iceland exhales. The puffins return to Borgarfjörður Eystri. The humpbacks come up to feed in Skjálfandi Bay. Snow pulls back from the lowland roads and the interior wakes slowly, cautiously, the way a bear might. For three weeks between the last of the winter closures and the full arrival of summer, the island is briefly, almost impossibly, yours.

This route loops the Ring Road counterclockwise, weighted toward the volcanic northeast — where Lake Mývatn steams beneath pseudocraters and the earth is still trying to decide whether to cool — and the glacial southeast, where icebergs calve into a tidal lagoon and wash back onto a beach of black sand. Two nights at Mývatn. Two in the Eastfjords. Two at the glacier. The sunsets last four hours. The light does not go out.

Around those anchors, smaller rooms unfold: a black wooden church on a lava peninsula, a rainbow-painted art village in a fjord, a lava-field cabin under the midnight sun, a concrete hotel at the edge of the continental rift. Twelve nights. Not a list. A passage.

CHAPTER 0 · PRACTICAL

Two apps, and you're set.

Iceland charges for parking at most tourist sites and in central Reykjavík. Two apps handle 95% of it. Download both before you board, register your rental plate, and add a card.

Tourist sites · 45+ locations

Parka

The default for nature reserves, waterfalls, canyons, and most tourist car parks outside Reykjavík. Tickets are typically 1000 ISK (~$7) and valid until midnight at most sites. Register your rental plate once; pay in 10 seconds per stop.

iOS App Store → Google Play → parka.app →
Towns · Reykjavík · Akureyri

EasyPark

Pay-by-minute for street parking in Reykjavík (zones P1–P4) and other towns. Best for short stops where you'd rather not overestimate. Reykjavík P1 is 630 ISK/hr; P3/P4 only 230 ISK/hr.

iOS App Store → Google Play → easypark.com →
Don't ignore a ticket. A standard meter violation is 4,500 ISK (~$37), discounted to 3,400 ISK if you pay immediately. Illegal parking is 10,000 ISK. Blue Car Rental will pass on any unpaid fine plus a ~2,000 ISK handling fee, and they will charge your card. Cheaper to spend 30 seconds in the app.

REYKJAVÍK · ARRIVAL

The saga, beginning.

Wheels touch asphalt at Keflavík at the hour when the day is already thinking about evening. Out the window: black lava fields, an airport the size of a regional terminal, an island that will not get dark tonight. By noon the rental 4WD is loaded; by mid-afternoon, the city.

Reykjavík is small enough to walk in a single afternoon — pastel row houses, a concert hall that looks like broken glass, a lutheran cathedral built to resemble basalt columns. But today is not a walking day. Today is a check in, breathe out, remember you are here day.

SNÆFELLSNES · BÚÐIR

A black church on a lava field.

North out of the city, around a fjord that once held a US naval base, past a tunnel cut beneath the sea, and onto a peninsula Jules Verne set Journey to the Center of the Earth on. At the end of the road, a sweep of moss-covered lava ending at the Atlantic — and alone upon it, the seventeenth-century church of Búðir. Painted black. Always has been.

The hotel beside it is the only dinner for forty kilometres in any direction. Book your table when you check in. Then walk the lava path to the church while the sun is still high — this far north in May, "sunset" happens at 22:00 and lingers.

NORTH · AKUREYRI

The long north.

This is the day you cross. Five hours in the driver's seat, tracing the northern coast from west to east. Iceland narrows here. The Atlantic pressing from one side, uninhabited highlands from the other. You will pass more sheep than cars.

Break the drive twice. First at Glaumbær — a turf-roofed farm from the saga era. Then at Hvítserkur, a basalt sea stack local tradition says is a petrified troll, caught out by the sunrise. Arrive Akureyri — unofficial capital of the north, population 19,000 — with traffic lights shaped like hearts.

NORTHEAST · LAKE MÝVATN

Where the earth is still smoking.

Mývatn is not a lake so much as a wound the earth has mostly healed from. Shallow water studded with pseudocraters. Sulphur fields hissing and hot to the touch. Mud pots clicking like old radiators. A lava castle you can walk through. The densest concentration of must-see geology anywhere in Iceland — and worth every hour of these two nights.

Late May means the sun dips below the horizon for about ninety minutes at three in the morning, and barely that. The midges — the namesake of the lake, mý + vatn — have just begun to return. Bring a head net.

EAST FJORDS · FÁSKRÚÐSFJÖRÐUR

Basalt, solitude, and a rainbow road.

East of Mývatn the road climbs through a pass of black gravel and dropping snow. On the far side, fjords — long fingers of Atlantic cut deep into the basalt — and Iceland's least-visited region. Two nights here is not a compromise; it is a reward. The part of Iceland most travellers rush through and later wish they hadn't.

On the way in, Stuðlagil canyon — hexagonal basalt columns lining a glacier-fed river the colour of a swimming pool. On day two, Seyðisfjörður — a rainbow-painted village in a fjord so steep the clouds sit below the ridgeline. Between them, the quietest roads on the Ring, fishing harbours nobody else visits, and twenty hours of daylight with nothing to do but look.

SOUTHEAST · VATNAJÖKULL

Ice meeting black sand.

This is why you came. Vatnajökull — the largest glacier in Europe — sends frozen tongues down to the coast, where they calve into a tidal lagoon called Jökulsárlón. The icebergs drift. Seals ride them. At the far end the lagoon empties under a narrow bridge and spits the ice onto a beach made of black volcanic sand. The contrast does not look real, even when you are standing on it.

Two nights close to the lagoon itself. You will need every hour.

SOUTH · KIRKJUBÆJARKLAUSTUR

Cabins on a moss-covered lava field.

A two-hour drive west brings you to Kirkjubæjarklaustur — a name so long Icelanders shorten it to "Klaustur" — where moss has crept for nine centuries over a lava flow the size of a small country, and where twenty-five modern cabins have been set into it like stones on a board. Magma. No tour buses stop here. The restaurant is quietly excellent. The hot tub faces the moss.

Stop en route at Fjaðrárgljúfur — a hundred-metre-deep canyon carved by a glacial river. A viewing platform. Twenty minutes. You will not forget it.

GOLDEN CIRCLE · NESJAVELLIR

Concrete, glass, and steam.

The longest, most beautiful driving day — and the one that ends at the architectural exclamation point of the whole trip. You will hit every iconic south-coast waterfall before lunch. You will stand where the Eurasian and North American plates pull apart. You will finish in a concrete cube on stilts at the edge of a rift valley lake, with a Northern Lights bar and a geothermal pool.

Tomorrow you fly home. Tonight there is no packing. Only soaking.
